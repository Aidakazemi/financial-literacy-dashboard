Section 1

The State of Financial Literacy in America

Research Question: What share of Americans demonstrate basic financial knowledge, and how has this changed over 15 years of measurement?

Financial Literacy Rate Over Time
% adults scoring 5+ of 7 on FINRA's Big-7 knowledge quiz. After 12 years of improvement, 2024 marks a concerning new low.
Source: FINRA Foundation NFCS 2009–2024
Knowledge by Topic Area
Which financial concepts do Americans understand most — and least? Bond pricing and risk diversification remain the largest knowledge gaps.
Source: FINRA Foundation NFCS 2024
📉 Historic Low
Only 27% of adults answered 5 or more of 7 financial knowledge questions correctly in 2024 — the lowest rate since measurement began in 2009 and down from 42% in 2009.
⚠ Struggle of the Middle
Middle-income households ($25k–$75k), those with some college but no degree, and adults 35–54 show disproportionate financial strain — despite stable incomes.
📱 Inflation Awareness Up
Correct responses to the inflation question rose from 53% to 58% between 2021 and 2024 — possibly reflecting lived experience with elevated prices.
💸 $415B Annual Cost
The National Financial Educators Council estimates financial illiteracy costs Americans $415 billion per year — roughly $1M per minute — through poor decisions, fees, and lost returns.

Who Is Most Financially Vulnerable?

Research Question: How do financial literacy and fragility vary by age, education, income, and race — and which demographic intersections face compounding disadvantages?

Literacy & Fragility by Age Group
Younger adults have the lowest financial knowledge AND the highest financial fragility — a compounding vulnerability at the start of their wealth-building years.
Source: FINRA Foundation NFCS 2024
Education's Dual Dividend
Education predicts both financial literacy AND retirement savings participation. The gap between college graduates and non-graduates is 43 percentage points in retirement savings.
Source: FINRA Foundation NFCS 2024
Income Gradient in Literacy & Fragility
Financial fragility drops sharply with income — but even households earning $50k–75k show significant vulnerability, with 42% unable to cover a $2,000 emergency.
Source: FINRA Foundation NFCS 2024
Literacy by Race / Ethnicity
Significant racial gaps persist in measured financial literacy — reflecting structural inequities in educational access, wealth accumulation, and financial system inclusion.
Source: FINRA Foundation NFCS 2024

Financial Fragility, Savings & Credit Behavior

Research Question: How financially resilient are American households, and what patterns of savings and credit use signal structural vulnerability?

Financial Fragility Trend (2009–2024)
After 12 years of improvement, fragility reversed sharply in 2024. 42% of adults say they could not cover a $2,000 emergency within 30 days — matching 2015 levels.
Source: FINRA Foundation NFCS 2009–2024
Credit Card Payment Behavior
20% of card holders pay only the minimum — a costly habit that compounds debt. Only 45% consistently pay their full balance monthly.
Source: FINRA Foundation NFCS 2024

Section 4

Wealth Inequality & Retirement Gaps

Research Question: How stark are racial and generational wealth disparities in the U.S., and what do they imply about retirement security?

Median Net Worth by Race/Ethnicity
White families hold 6.5× the median net worth of Black families and 5.6× that of Hispanic families — a structural gap that persists across income levels.
Source: Federal Reserve Survey of Consumer Finances 2022
Median Retirement Savings by Age
Median retirement balances peak in the 65–74 cohort at $200k — far below the $1M–$1.5M many advisors recommend. Adults under 35 have a median of just $18k saved.
Source: Federal Reserve Survey of Consumer Finances 2022
🏠 6.5× Wealth Gap
White families' median net worth ($285k) is 6.5× that of Black families ($44k). This gap has persisted for decades and cannot be explained by income differences alone.
👴 Retirement Crisis
32% of working-age Americans have zero retirement savings. Among Millennials specifically, 66% have no retirement savings — despite being in prime earning years.
🎓 Education Dividend
80% of college graduates have a retirement account, vs. 37% of those without any college experience — a 43-point gap that compounds over a lifetime.

Banking Access, Gaps & FinTech Adoption

Research Question: Who remains excluded from the formal financial system, and does FinTech adoption narrow or widen the literacy-access gap?

Unbanked Rate by Household Income
Lack of banking access is strongly income-stratified: 15% of households earning below $15k are unbanked vs. under 1% above $50k — compounding financial exclusion at the bottom.
Source: FDIC National Survey of Unbanked & Underbanked 2023
FinTech & Digital Tool Adoption (2024)
Mobile banking is nearly universal (81%), but newer tools like robo-advisors (11%) and BNPL (28%) show uneven adoption — raising questions about who benefits from FinTech innovation.
Source: FINRA Foundation NFCS 2024

Section 6

Macro Financial Environment

Research Question: How do aggregate household debt, savings rates, and cost-of-living trends shape the context in which financial literacy deficits do the most damage?

U.S. Personal Savings Rate
The savings rate has fallen well below its historical average (~7%). Live data from FRED (BEA). When this is low, financial illiteracy translates directly into household vulnerability.
Source: FRED — BEA Personal Saving Rate (PSAVERT) · Weekly auto-refresh
Household Debt Service as % of Income
The share of disposable income consumed by debt repayments. Live data from FRED. Rising debt burden reduces the margin for financial error among less-literate households.
Source: FRED — Federal Reserve Debt Service Payments (TDSP) · Weekly auto-refresh
Credit Card Delinquency Rate
Share of credit card loans 90+ days past due. Rising delinquency is a direct consequence of low financial literacy — particularly among minimum-payment-only cardholders.
Source: FRED — DRCCLACBS · Weekly auto-refresh
Federal Funds Rate
The policy rate directly affects borrowing costs for mortgages, credit cards, and student loans. Financial literacy around interest rates becomes more consequential in high-rate environments.
Source: FRED — FEDFUNDS · Weekly auto-refresh
